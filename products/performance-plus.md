## What Does The Research Say?

Creatine monohydrate is the most extensively researched ergogenic aid in sports nutrition, with its primary benefit centered on increasing intramuscular phosphocreatine stores, which directly fuels ATP resynthesis during maximal, short-duration efforts. A landmark meta-analysis published in 2003 found that creatine supplementation increased maximum strength by approximately 8% and weightlifting performance by 14% compared to placebo groups [PMID 12701815](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12701815/). Creatine is particularly effective for repeated sprint performance, with studies showing a 15â20% improvement in peak power output during consecutive high-intensity bouts.

Beta-alanine at 3.2g per day â the clinically validated dose â works by elevating muscle carnosine concentrations, which serves as an intracellular buffer against the accumulation of hydrogen ions (Hâº) during intense exercise. A pivotal randomized controlled trial by Hill and colleagues (2007) demonstrated that 4â5g/day of beta-alanine over 10 weeks increased muscle carnosine content by up to 80% in the vastus lateralis, leading to significant improvements in exercise capacity [PMID 17131003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17131003/). A comprehensive meta-analysis covering 15 studies and 360 participants confirmed that beta-alanine significantly improved exercise outcomes lasting 1â4 minutes â the time domain most relevant to resistance training sets and high-intensity intervals â with an overall effect size of 0.374 [PMID 22727960](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22727960/). L-Glutamine at 2g per day supports recovery by replenishing the most abundant free amino acid in skeletal muscle, which becomes significantly depleted during prolonged or intense exercise. Research by Castell and Newsholme (1997) demonstrated that glutamine supplementation in endurance athletes reduced the incidence of infections post-marathon by modulating immune function and supporting gut integrity under exercise stress [PMID 9263248](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9263248/). A study by Legault and colleagues (2015) found that glutamine supplementation attenuated muscle soreness and reduced markers of muscle damage (creatine kinase levels) following eccentric exercise, with subjects reporting faster return to baseline strength within 72 hours [PMID 25811544](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25811544/). While glutamine's direct anabolic effects are modest compared to creatine, its role in maintaining gut barrier function, nitrogen balance, and immune competency makes it a valuable recovery adjunct.

The safety profiles for all three ingredients in PERFORMANCE PLUS are exceptionally well-characterized. ## Mechanism of Action

The mechanism is: Creatine monohydrate increases intramuscular phosphocreatine stores, accelerating the regeneration of adenosine triphosphate (ATP) via the creatine kinase enzyme pathway during maximal-intensity contractions, thereby sustaining peak power output across multiple efforts. Beta-alanine is rate-limiting for carnosine synthesis in skeletal muscle; by elevating intramuscular carnosine concentrations, it buffers the acidic environment created by Hâº accumulation during glycolytic metabolism, delaying the onset of muscular fatigue and extending high-intensity work capacity. L-Glutamine supports nitrogen retention, mTOR-mediated anabolic signaling in myocytes, and intestinal mucosal integrity, collectively improving the recovery milieu following exercise-induced muscle damage.
