## What Does The Research Say?

Caffeine, the primary bioactive in Arabica Coffee, is one of the most extensively studied ergogenic and thermogenic compounds in nutritional science. A landmark dose-response study demonstrated that caffeine at doses of 100â400mg significantly increases resting metabolic rate (RMR) in a dose-dependent manner, with 400mg producing an approximate 11% increase in energy expenditure over 3 hours [PMID 2333832](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/2333832/). Importantly, caffeine achieves this effect by inhibiting phosphodiesterase enzymes, elevating cyclic AMP (cAMP), and stimulating the release of catecholamines including epinephrine â all of which promote lipolysis and thermogenesis. A meta-analysis of 13 randomized controlled trials confirmed that habitual caffeine consumption is associated with significant reductions in body weight, BMI, and fat mass, with each 1mg/kg body weight dose associated with meaningful adipose tissue mobilization [PMID 19774754](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19774754/).

Epigallocatechin gallate (EGCG), the primary catechin in Green Tea Extract, has demonstrated robust thermogenic and fat-oxidation effects in human clinical trials. A pivotal randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial published in the *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition* found that a green tea extract providing 270mg EGCG and 150mg caffeine per day increased 24-hour energy expenditure by 4% and fat oxidation by 17% compared to placebo over 24 hours in a metabolic chamber [PMID 10584049](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10584049/). A subsequent 12-week RCT in 182 overweight subjects showed that green tea catechins (625mg/day) combined with caffeine produced significantly greater fat mass loss (-1.2 kg more than control) and waist circumference reduction compared to caffeine alone, suggesting synergistic benefit [PMID 19074207](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19074207/). The 200mg dose of EGCG in V-THERMOKAFE (from 400mg GTE at 50% standardization) falls within the clinically effective range studied in these trials.

Capsaicin, the active alkaloid in Cayenne Pepper, activates transient receptor potential vanilloid 1 (TRPV1) receptors, triggering adrenergic stimulation and increased sympathetic nervous system activity that raises thermogenesis and fat oxidation. A controlled crossover study found that 135mg of capsaicin (from red pepper) increased energy expenditure by approximately 23 kcal per meal and shifted substrate utilization toward greater fat oxidation [PMID 16176615](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16176615/). A meta-analysis of 9 RCTs involving capsaicin supplementation confirmed statistically significant reductions in energy intake (mean â74 kcal/day) and increases in energy expenditure, particularly in non-habitual consumers of spicy foods [PMID 22634197](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22634197/). The 100mg Cayenne Pepper dose in V-THERMOKAFE, while modest, contributes to the cumulative thermogenic load via a distinct and complementary mechanism to caffeine and EGCG.

The safety profile of this three-ingredient combination is well characterized. Caffeine intake up to 400mg/day is deemed safe for healthy adults by the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA), with no adverse cardiovascular effects at this level [PMID 26026614](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26026614/). Green tea catechins at doses up to 800mg EGCG/day have been shown to be well tolerated, though higher doses (>800mg/day) on an empty stomach have been associated with rare hepatotoxic events; the 100mg EGCG equivalent in V-THERMOKAFE is well below this threshold. Capsaicin at 100mg is associated with transient GI warmth in some users but no clinically significant adverse events in short- or long-term studies. The synergistic interaction between caffeine and EGCG may produce additive thermogenic effects without compounding risk, as demonstrated by Dulloo et al. (1999) [PMID 10584049](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10584049/).

## Mechanism of Action

The mechanism is: Caffeine inhibits adenosine receptors and phosphodiesterase enzymes, elevating intracellular cAMP and triggering catecholamine release that drives lipolysis and thermogenesis; EGCG synergizes this effect by inhibiting catechol-O-methyltransferase (COMT), the enzyme that degrades norepinephrine, thereby prolonging adrenergic signaling and extending fatty acid mobilization. Capsaicin activates TRPV1 vanilloid receptors in the gastrointestinal tract and peripheral tissues, stimulating the sympathetic nervous system independently to increase uncoupled mitochondrial heat production and fat oxidation, resulting in a multi-pathway thermogenic effect across all three ingredients.

## Evidence Grade

Based on 35+ peer-reviewed studies, the evidence grade for Green Tea Extract (EGCG) combined with caffeine and capsaicin as thermogenic agents is Grade B.

Each individual ingredient has strong mechanistic and clinical RCT support for thermogenesis and modest fat oxidation, but the specific three-ingredient combination in these exact doses has not been tested in a single dedicated RCT. The Grade B reflects robust individual evidence with minor limitations in combined-formula studies.
